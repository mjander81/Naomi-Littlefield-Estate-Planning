Estate Planning Basics

Regardless of unique circumstances, I recommend everyone at least take the following steps:


  1. Designate beneficiaries on accounts, life insurance, and vehicles
  2. Prepare and sign a will, financial power of attorney, and advance directive for health
  3. Consider using a revocable living trust to avoid probate

Benefits of Beneficiary Designations

Beneficiary designations enable an asset to pass to a beneficiary upon your death without going through probate, which is a complex and often costly legal process.


You can designate one or more beneficiary or payable upon death name on bank accounts, stock and investment accounts, retirement accounts, and life insurance. If you own a vehicle that does not have a loan, you can also designate a beneficiary for the vehicle through the Maryland Department of Transportation.


Benefits of a Having Will

Having a will enables you to:


  • Appoint the legal guardian for your minor child or children
  • Appoint the person who will administer your probate estate
  • Determine who will inherit assets in your probate estate (within the limits of the augmented spousal election share)

If you do not have a will when you die, the Maryland laws of intestacy will determine who has priority to serve as your personal representative and who will inherit your probate assets.


Benefits of Having a Financial Power of Attorney Document

A Financial Power of Attorney Document will allow someone else to step in and manage your finances in the event that you are not able to manage them yourself. In the case of a medical emergency, it is much better to have this prepared in advance versus trying to complete it while in a hospital. Also, some people temporarily or permanently lose the mental capacity to complete the document, so it is good to complete it well in advance of there being any risk of that happening.


Benefits of Having an Advance Directive for Health

An Advance Directive for Health will allow someone else to make medical decisions for you in the event that you are not able to make them for yourself. In addition to designating the person who has the legal right to do this for you, the document enables you to express your desires for care (a) when death is imminent and also (b) in the event you are in a persistent vegetative state.


Benefits of Putting Real Estate in a Revocable Living Trust

Putting your home and any other real property that you own into a revocable living trust prior to your death would likely save the person managing your estate after your death a lot of time and also make the administration of your estate much less expensive. A revocable living trust enables a successor trustee to transfer the ownership of your property, upon your death and pursuant to your instructions, without having to go through probate.


When a person puts their property into a revocable living trust for the purpose of estate planning, what they are doing from a technical legal perspective is changing the ownership of the property from conventional ownership to owning the property as a trustee, for their own benefit during their life-time, and for the benefit of their beneficiaries upon their death. The word “revocable” in revocable living trust means that the person who put the property in the trust can take the property out of the trust.


Probate: Why does it make sense to keep property out of probate?

Probate is the distribution of property pursuant to a will or under the Maryland laws of intestacy. It is a formal legal process overseen by Maryland's Orphan's court. The first step in the probate process is the person appointed as your personal representative in your will must petition the Court to formally be appointed to serve as your personal representative. There are several additional steps that must be taken before your probate estate can be distributed. If your personal representative is not a resident of the state of Maryland, they must designate an in-state agent. A personal representative is entitled to about 4% of the probate estate as compensation for serving in this role. Because probate is complex, a personal representative often has an attorney assist. Typically, an attorney will charge at least half of the amount that the personal representative is entitled to, which results in the attorney receiving about 2% of the probate estate. Keeping property out of probate will save your loved ones both time and money.


Maryland laws of intestacy: What happens if you die without a will?

Maryland laws of intestacy: What happens if you die without a will? If you do not have a will when you die, the Maryland laws of intestacy will determine who has priority to serve as your personal representative and who will inherit your probate assets. Here is how your probate estate will be distributed:


  1. Spouse and minor children: Spouse receives one-half and children share the remaining one-half.
  2. Spouse and adult children: Spouse receives $40,000 plus one-half of remaining estate, children divide balance with the interest of a predeceased child passing to issue of that child.
  3. Children only: Children (does not include step-children) divide entire estate with the interest of a predeceased child passing to issue of that child.
  4. Spouse and parents: If married less than five years, spouse receives $40,000 plus one-half of remaining estate and parents divide balance or surviving parent takes balance. If married more than 5 years, see #5.
  5. Spouse of the decedent without other heirs listed above: Spouse receives entire estate.
  6. Parents of the decedent without other heirs listed above: Parents divide entire estate or surviving parent takes all.
  7. Siblings of the decedent without heirs listed above: Siblings divide estate equally, with the share of a deceased sibling going to their issue.
  8. Grandparents without other heirs listed above: Grandparents divide entire estate or, if deceased, to their issue.
  9. Great-grandparent without other heirs listed above: Great-grandparents divide entire estate or, if deceased, to their issue.
  10. Step-children: If there are no heirs listed above, step-children divide estate equally.
  11. No living heirs or step-children: If decedent was a recipient of long-term care benefits under the Maryland Medical Assistance Program at time of death, net estate is paid to Department of Health and Mental Hygiene. Otherwise, the net estate is paid to the Board of Education.

Augmented spousal election share: How does marriage impact estate distribution?

Regardless of what your will and beneficiary designations provide, Maryland law allows a surviving spouse to claim one-third of your estate if you have surviving children or one-half if you do not have surviving children. This election applies to your entire estate, not just your probate estate, although courts do have discretion to exclude assets based on the nature of the marital relationship. The spousal election share can be waived through a prenuptial or postnuptial agreement.
